# Cypriot Collaborator Search Playbook

Companion to
[`superpowers/specs/2026-04-26-business-concept-audit.md`](./superpowers/specs/2026-04-26-business-concept-audit.md)
§7 recommendation #2.

The single biggest unlock for DROMOS is partnering with a Cypriot
collaborator before further build or validation work. The strategy doc
itself names this as the top mitigation in its risks section. Without it,
union meetings, Ministry of Transport pre-clearance, hotel-concierge
sales, and driver-rank fieldwork are all materially harder or
impossible.

This document covers:

1. What "collaborator" means here
2. Search channels (ranked by yield)
3. Selection criteria
4. Outreach templates (English + Greek)
5. Equity / engagement structures to offer
6. Red flags

---

## 1. What "collaborator" means

Three roles, in descending order of preference:

- **Co-founder.** Full equity stake (20–40% depending on stage and
  contribution). Splits the work. The ideal outcome.
- **Operating advisor with vested equity.** 1–5% over 2–4 years, paid in
  monthly time commitment (typically 4–8 hours/week). They open doors,
  attend the union meeting, make introductions, do not run the
  business.
- **Local agent / consultant.** Paid hourly or per-deliverable. They
  represent the company in-country for a fee. No equity. Lowest-trust
  relationship; useful as a stop-gap, not a long-term answer.

If at all possible: aim for co-founder. The strategy doc's cultural
analysis suggests the *appearance* of an outsider running things will
hurt the business, and an advisor relationship can read that way.

## 2. Search channels — ranked by likely yield

### Tier 1 — High yield

- **Cypriot diaspora networks abroad** (London, Birmingham, Athens,
  Thessaloniki, Tel Aviv). Many second-generation Cypriots want a
  reason to spend more time on the island; a startup with traction is
  a compelling one. Search:
  - LinkedIn filter: location: Cyprus OR location: London + previous
    location: Cyprus + interests: startups OR transport
  - Diaspora associations: National Federation of Cypriots in the UK,
    Cypriot Brotherhood
  - Greek/Cypriot university alumni groups (King's, LSE, Imperial,
    UCL)

- **Cypriot tech ecosystem** — small but networked. Two key entry
  points:
  - **Cyprus Seeds / Limassol Startups** community
  - **IDEA Innovation Center** (Bank of Cyprus's incubator, Nicosia)
  - Conferences: Reflect Festival, TechIsland Summit
  - LinkedIn search: Cyprus + "founder" / "CEO" / "operator"

- **Cypriot tourism / hospitality industry insiders.** Concierges,
  hotel managers, DMC operators. They understand the demand side
  (which is where DROMOS is weakest) and they know taxi drivers
  professionally.

### Tier 2 — Medium yield

- **University placement programmes.** University of Cyprus (UCY) and
  Cyprus University of Technology (CUT) have entrepreneurship clubs and
  final-year project supervisors who maintain alumni lists. Useful for
  finding younger collaborators, less useful for establishing
  credibility with 50+ taxi drivers.

- **Reddit r/cyprus + Greek-language forums.** Lower-quality leads but
  occasionally surfaces motivated individuals. Use for awareness, not
  primary recruiting.

- **Facebook expat / SMB groups.** Cypriot SMB life happens on
  Facebook. Useful for sensing the business culture and identifying
  individuals who post regularly about local entrepreneurship.

### Tier 3 — Last resort

- **Cold outreach to Cypriot taxi cooperatives or fleet operators.**
  Likely to read as a sales pitch and burn the relationship.
  Approach only after you have something to demo.

- **Recruitment agencies in Cyprus.** Expensive, slow, mismatched
  for early-stage startup roles. Skip.

## 3. Selection criteria

Optimise for these, in order:

1. **Cultural credibility on a Cypriot taxi rank.** Speaks Cypriot
   Greek (not just standard Greek). Has family or friends in the
   transport industry. Comfortable spending two hours drinking coffee
   at the LCA rank without it being weird.
2. **Operating bias over thinking bias.** This phase needs
   conversations, meetings, and follow-up emails — not strategy decks.
3. **Industry-adjacent network.** Hospitality, transport, tourism,
   Cyprus regulatory affairs. Someone who already knows a hotel GM is
   worth 10 someones who would have to be introduced to one.
4. **Time availability.** A part-time CFO with a full-time job at PwC
   Cyprus is less useful than someone who can spend a Tuesday morning
   at the rank.
5. **Risk tolerance.** Equity-for-time deals require people who can
   wait 3+ years for liquidity.
6. **English fluency for ongoing async collaboration.** Critical if the
   founder is working remotely in another timezone.

Things that are nice but not load-bearing:

- Prior startup experience.
- Technical skills — the codebase is already overbuilt; the gap is
  market-side.
- A degree from a top-tier school. Irrelevant on the LCA rank.

## 5. Engagement structures to offer

| Role | Equity | Vesting | Time commitment | Cash |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Co-founder (full) | 25–40% | 4 yr / 1 yr cliff | Full-time within 6 mo | None pre-revenue |
| Co-founder (part-time → full-time) | 20–30% | 4 yr / 1 yr cliff, milestone-tied | 8–16 hr/wk now → FT later | None |
| Operating advisor | 1–3% | 2 yr / no cliff | 4–8 hr/wk | None |
| Paid country-manager | 0.5–1% | 2 yr | Full-time | Market salary (€36–60k) |

Be ready to negotiate. Cypriot business culture treats equity-only
relationships with more skepticism than the SF / London tech scene
does. Expect to either pay something cash-wise once an MVP is live or
to demonstrate clear personal financial commitment to the project.

## 6. Red flags

Walk away if you see:

- **A "rainmaker" pitch.** Someone who claims they can deliver "all
  the hotels in Ayia Napa" within a month. This is almost never true,
  and the people who say it are usually selling, not partnering.
- **Demand for cash up front before any work.** Different from a
  market-rate salary; this is a transaction-fee on access. Pass.
- **Inability or unwillingness to go to a taxi rank.** This is the
  job. If they are too senior, too busy, or too sales-focused to
  spend two hours at LCA, they are not the right fit, no matter how
  good their network looks on paper.
- **Vague references to "Cyprus connections" without specifics.** Ask
  for two named people they could introduce you to within 7 days. If
  they can't name them, the network is theoretical.
- **No interest in the strategy doc, only in equity terms.** They are
  optimising for ownership, not for the work.

## 7. Cadence

A 60-day target for finding a collaborator is realistic if it is
treated as a primary activity, not a side task.

Suggested rhythm:

- **Week 1:** assemble candidate list (target: 25 names across all
  channels).
- **Weeks 2–4:** outreach in waves of 5–8 messages per week. Track
  responses in a private spreadsheet.
- **Weeks 3–6:** first calls. Aim for 10–15 first conversations.
- **Weeks 5–8:** second meetings with the strongest 2–3 candidates.
  Take them to a taxi rank.
- **Weeks 7–9:** equity / role discussions with the final candidate.
- **Week 10:** documented agreement (founder-agreement template;
  Cypriot-law-friendly).

If by week 10 there is no agreement: extend by 30 days, broaden the
search to Tier 2 channels, or seriously consider whether the
project's shape needs to change (license-and-sell to a Cypriot
operator vs. build-and-run).

---

## 8. Tracking

A simple private tracker is enough — not in this repo, not in
Supabase. Examples of what to capture per candidate:

- Name, source channel, first-contact date
- Stage (no response / first call done / second call done /
  taxi-rank visit done / offer made)
- Notes from each conversation
- Why they would or would not work, in your honest words

This is the most important file in the project for the next 60
days. Treat it accordingly.
